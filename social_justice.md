# Computational Social Justice: Cancer Mortality Disparities Among Black Women in Georgia

## The Problem: Structural Inequality in Cancer Outcomes

Cancer mortality in the United States has declined over time, yet these improvements have not been experienced equally across all populations. Black women, in particular, continue to face disproportionately higher cancer mortality rates compared to White women. This project focuses on Black women in Georgia as a marginalized population affected by persistent health inequities rooted in structural racism, unequal access to healthcare, and systemic barriers within medical institutions.

The injustice addressed in this project is not simply a matter of individual health outcomes, but a broader issue of healthcare inequality. Black women are more likely to be diagnosed at later stages of cancer, less likely to receive timely or adequate treatment, and more likely to experience barriers such as lack of insurance, provider bias, and limited access to high-quality care facilities. These disparities are well-documented. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), Black women have higher death rates for most major cancers despite sometimes having lower incidence rates. Similarly, the American Cancer Society reports that Black women are approximately 40% more likely to die from breast cancer than White women. The National Institutes of Health (NIH) further highlights that structural and social determinants of health—including income, environment, and access to care—play a major role in these outcomes.

These patterns are not random; they are the result of long-standing systemic inequities that shape health outcomes over time. Understanding these disparities requires not only data analysis but also a recognition that data reflects broader social realities.

## The Findings: Data as Evidence of Disparity

The statistical and machine learning analyses conducted in this project provide empirical evidence that supports the existence of cancer mortality disparities between Black and White women in Georgia. Descriptive statistics and visualizations consistently showed higher age-adjusted mortality rates for Black women across cancer types and time periods. Even when overall mortality rates declined slightly between 2016–2020 and 2020–2024, the gap between Black and White women remained.

A formal hypothesis test (t-test) confirmed that the difference in mortality rates between Black and White women is statistically significant. This means the observed disparities are unlikely to be due to random variation and instead reflect a consistent pattern in the data.

The machine learning models further reinforced these findings. Predictive modeling showed that variables related to race and mortality patterns are strong predictors of cancer outcomes. Feature importance analysis revealed that mortality rates and death counts associated with Black women played a significant role in predicting outcomes, indicating that disparities are embedded within the structure of the data itself.

At the county level, the disparities become even more pronounced. For example, counties such as DeKalb show significantly higher death counts among Black women compared to White women, despite both populations living within the same geographic region. This highlights how localized inequities persist even within broader state-level trends.

However, it is also important to acknowledge the limitations of the dataset. The data is aggregated and does not capture individual-level factors such as income, insurance status, or access to specific healthcare providers. Additionally, the dataset focuses on recorded mortality outcomes, which may not fully reflect underlying issues such as underdiagnosis or disparities in reporting. These limitations suggest that the true extent of inequality may be even greater than what is observed.

## Advocacy & Recommendations: Moving from Data to Action

The findings of this project clearly demonstrate that cancer mortality disparities among Black women are not only present but persistent. Addressing this issue requires action at multiple levels, including policy, healthcare systems, and community engagement.

First, there must be increased investment in equitable healthcare access. This includes expanding access to cancer screening, improving early detection programs in predominantly Black communities, and ensuring that high-quality treatment facilities are geographically and financially accessible. Mobile screening units, community health partnerships, and targeted outreach programs can help bridge gaps in access.

Second, healthcare systems must address implicit bias and structural barriers within care delivery. Training programs for healthcare providers, along with accountability measures, can help reduce disparities in diagnosis and treatment. Additionally, improving data collection to include more detailed demographic and socioeconomic variables would allow for more precise identification of at-risk populations.

Third, policymakers should prioritize resource allocation based on need. Counties with the highest disparities, such as DeKalb, should receive targeted funding and intervention programs. Data-driven decision-making can ensure that resources are directed where they will have the greatest impact.

Finally, there must be a continued emphasis on research and transparency. Data storytelling and public-facing dashboards, such as those developed in this project, play a critical role in making disparities visible and understandable. When inequities are clearly presented, it becomes more difficult to ignore them.

The human cost of inaction is significant. Behind every data point is a life lost, a family affected, and a community impacted. Persistent disparities in cancer mortality mean that Black women continue to face unequal chances of survival, not because of biology alone, but because of systemic inequities that shape access to care and health outcomes.

This project demonstrates that data can do more than describe inequality—it can expose it, quantify it, and ultimately support efforts to address it. The responsibility now lies with stakeholders to act on this evidence and work toward a more equitable healthcare system.
